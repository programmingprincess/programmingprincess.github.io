StudyGenie: An intelligent and collaborative web-based study platform

Accepted to ACM's SIGCSE 2018 Student Research Competition, Baltimore, Maryland.

Study Genie is a web-based study tool that aims to explore the role of collaborative learning and note-taking on student performance in lower division computer science courses. Students may write their notes or code snippets in mini notecards, and then assemble them into a cheatsheet for use during an exam.

All student movements are tracked (i.e., notecard clicked, cheatsheet added, copying and pasting vs. typing), allowing my team to analyze and understand how students study, and then compare these statistics with overall student performance in the course and exams.

This application differs from others in that it allows students to share and manipulate their notes on a smaller, topic-based scale, in the form of mini notecards. Deployed on a Windows Server using IIS and HTTP Platform Handler.

Implemented Visualizations:

We produced visualizations split into two categories to represent two types of data: activity and content. Activity-based visualizations (usage breakdown and calendar) reflect a student's actions and behavior while using the application; content-based visualizations (topic frequencies) build models around note content to supplement student learning experience.

Skills utilized: Java, SQL, D3.js, HTML/CSS, JavaScript/jQuery, and more

FURI: Synthetic Biology Research

The Fulton Undergraduate Research Symposium at ASU takes place every semester. Hundreds of grant-funded students present their research in the form of a poster to their peers, professors, faculty, and industry professionals. In Spring and Fall 2016, I presented my research on quorum sensing: Examining the natural diversity of quorum sensing for orthogonal pathways




Abstract: Homoserine lactone (HSL) quorum sensing (QS) is a method of bacterial cell-to-cell communication. Bioengineers have incorporated QS into biological circuits, using the bacteria’s signaling pathway as a “bio-wire” to connect steps in logical operations. Complex genetic circuitry is inhibited by pathway overlap, or “crosstalk,” and lack of pathways (only four out of 100s have been used in synthetic systems to date). To fill this gap, we have designed a decoupled system to test pathways for orthogonality (lack of crosstalk) using a sender cell, which carries the HSL synthase, and receiver cell, which carries the QS promoter and regulator. Our research will ultimately expand the synthetic biology toolbox and provide engineers with more flexibility when building complex biological circuitry.


TL;DR: We are looking for more orthogonal, or non-overlapping, quorum sensing communication pathwhays to use as a tool for bioengineers to build complex biological circuitry.


I have been working on this research since March 2015, at The Haynes Lab at ASU. We are working towards publishing our data in the near future.

Gilbert Fire Android App

This app was created by a team of three girls in March 2014, my senior year of high school. The app was submitted to the SPARK App Leage Mobile App Competition, partnered with Gilbert Fire Department and Intel.

The purpose of our app is to inform the citizens of Gilbert about their town's fire amenities and general fire safety tips. Since our audience ranges from children and teenagers to the elderly, our main focus was user-friendless and accessiblity.

We implemented a standard grid layout for the menu with Gilbert social media icons on the bottom.

A main feature of the app that I designed was the Google Maps location integration. This feature utilizes the Google Maps API to locate the fire station nearest the user's location. It also allows the user to see a list of fire stations in Gilbert, and their distance from the user's location.

The entire app was built using MIT App Inventor, a GUI that utilizes text-based building blocks to create mobile apps.

A modified version of the mobile application, inspired by our original app, is available in the Google Play Store!

Awards: "Best Overall App", "Girls in STEM"
